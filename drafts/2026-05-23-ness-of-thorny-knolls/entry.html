Ness of Thorny Knolls

Spring 2026

Ness has a name now: Ness of Thorny Knolls. Around here he is still Pumpkin's red bull calf first, but he is starting to look less like a calf we are watching and more like a young Highland with his own habits, his own routes, and his own ideas about where the best grass is hiding.

The easiest way to pick him out is his nose. Ness is the red calf with the pale nose. His sister can be close by in several of these pictures, and once the two of them get moving through the pasture or along the rocks, that pale nose is the quickest way to keep the story straight. Ness carries more red through his coat, and even when the spring growth is high around him, that face gives him away.

He is doing what good calves do in spring: testing the edges. He follows Pumpkin's lead, but he is not glued to her side anymore. He works the brush line, steps over the rocks, noses into the green places, and then circles back like he meant to check on everybody else all along. Pumpkin lets him stretch the line, then quietly gathers the shape of the day back around her.

There is a practical confidence to him. Ness does not rush every new thing. He studies it, decides whether it matters, and then keeps walking. In the pasture shots with his sister, he already has that steady square look that made him stand out as a young bull calf. In the closer pictures, he is all fringe, red coat, and pale nose, still soft with calf age but starting to show the frame underneath.

The rocks and trees have become part of his range. He moves through that edge like it is a little calf-sized obstacle course, sometimes with his sister nearby, sometimes on his own. Those are the pictures that feel most like this season: spring grass coming up fast, calves learning the map, and Pumpkin holding the center without making much fuss about it.

This draft also marks the name change on the site. The bull calf profile already points toward Ness, but future captions and journal entries should use his full name, Ness of Thorny Knolls, rather than the generic "bull calf." Where both calves appear together, the identification note should stay simple: Ness is red with the pale nose.

For now, he is still young enough that every week changes him. The legs get a little steadier, the coat fills out, the distance from Pumpkin gets a little longer, and the pale-nosed red calf becomes more clearly Ness.
